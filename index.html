Ivory: a Spatial Story in StoryMap.js

Ivory is an item rarely studied on its own, but this commodity proves useful as a vehicle to observe the context in which it was deemed valuable. In the late fifteenth and early sixteenth century, Portuguese merchants held a monopoly over trade in West Africa. They forced themselves into the population of the indigenous people in what is today known as Sierra Leone. There, they traded ivory and commissioned ivories, or pieces crafted from the material. The Portuguese appreciated and dutifully shipped these gifts, called ivories, to high-status people in Europe, commenting on the skill of the African carvers. Still, it was indigenous Africans being enslaved and valued for their physical labor, and it was indigenous labor and knowledge practices that produced the ivories so desired by high-status Europeans. With this is mind, the following map traces the path of travel for a piece of ivory. Ivory's journey from the living animal to human hands highlights the knowledge and skill required for indigenous West Africans to interact with the natural world, and explore how Sapi and Portuguese understood the existence of the other during the period of early contact of the sixteenth century.

The background for this story is a map of West Africa from 1743. The coastal areas and areas next to rivers are described with city or region names, while the interior of the continent is more blank and with more descriptions stating that area is unknown (incognita). In the bottom left corner, an African village is depicted and explained with Latin descriptions. In the village, piles of severed tusks are visible, and African children play on three pieces of ivory that have Italian, French, and German modes of measurement written on them.
